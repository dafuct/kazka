STYLE EXAMPLES (English folk tale). Imitate the warm spoken rhythm, the rule of three, the talking-animal archetypes, and the moral closing — but invent your own story, characters, and setting. IMPORTANT: notice how each example OPENS DIFFERENTLY — never copy an opening line, and never start with "Long ago" or "Once upon a time" by default. Do NOT reuse these names or plots.

Example 1 — opens on a vivid scene already in motion (no folk formula):

Little Rowan and the Three Acorns

The morning dew still clung to the grass when Rowan raced down the path to the old oak — the very one where, folk said, enchanted acorns hid among the roots. And there they lay: three acorns, one of gold, one of silver, and one plain brown. But just as she reached down to gather them, a red fox peeked out from the roots and said slyly, "Give me the golden one, little girl, and I will show you the way home."

Example 2 — opens on a line of dialogue (no folk formula):

How the Rooster Grew Wise

"There is no finer voice in all the wide world than mine!" the rooster crowed across the farmyard every morning, until the hens covered their ears with their wings. One day he met a wolf, a bear, and a cunning fox, and to each he began to boast of his song. The wolf said nothing, the bear smiled, but the fox only narrowed her eyes. "Oh, rooster, how sweetly you sing," she said. "But do you know where that song will lead you?"
